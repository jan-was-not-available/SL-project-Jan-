What Are the Rules in Boxing?

According to the Association of Boxing Commissions and Combative Sports (A.B.C.C.S), the first rule of boxing is: Each round (out of 12 total) will consist of 3 minutes, with a 1 minute break in between rounds. The second rule of Boxing is that the referee is the sole arbiter of a bout and is the only individual authorized to stop a contest. The third rule of Boxing is that all bouts will be evaluated and scored by three judges. The fourth rule in Boxing is that the 10 Point Must system will be the standard system of scoring a bout. The fifth rule is that the Mandatory (8) count after knockdowns will be standard procedure in all bouts. The sixth rule in boxing is all professional boxers are required to wear a mouthpiece during competition. The round cannot begin without the mouthpiece. If the mouthpiece is dislodged during competition, the referee will call time and have the mouthpiece replaced at the first opportune moment, without interfering with the immediate action. Points may be deducted by the referee if he feels the mouthpiece is being purposely spit out. The seventh rule is that there is NO standing eight count. The eighth rule is that there is NO (3) Knockdown rule. The 9th rule is a boxer shall receive a twenty (20) second count if the boxer is knocked out of the ring. The boxer is to be unassisted by spectators or his/her seconds. If assisted by anyone, the boxer may lose points or be disqualified with such a decision being within the sole discretion of the referee.

The History of Boxing and its Heroes.

Boxing's History is thousands of years long, and it is still evolving and being rewritten to this day. In its early stages Boxing was an olympic sport used to display battle without the use of any external weapons. Boxing did not have the rules it had today but it ensured that both challengers would only be using their glove-covered hands. The downside of the newborn sport though, is that there was no time limit, no weight classes or weight restrictions, and no scoring system. All of this just made it seem like a pointless fight to the death, and death was an exponentially higher likely outcome than it is with today's boxing rules. If you were an ancient boxer, the chances of you living a healthy life after starting your career were slim to none, with most boxers developing permanent paralysis or early signs of brain damage within days of their first fight.
